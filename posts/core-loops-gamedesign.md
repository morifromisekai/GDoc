# Core Loops: The Foundation of Game Design

Every game, from *Tetris* to *Elden Ring*, is built around a central engine called the **Core Loop**. 

A core loop is a sequence of actions that a player repeats throughout the entire game. Understanding, tuning, and expanding this loop is the single most important task of a game designer.

---

## The Three Components of a Core Loop

A classic core loop consists of three distinct phases:

1. **Action (The Player's Deed)**: The player performs an input. In a platformer, it's jumping over an obstacle. In an RPG, it's selecting an attack.
2. **Feedback (The Game's Response)**: The game rewards or penalizes the action. Coins clink, enemies flash red, numbers fly out, or the character takes damage.
3. **Motivation (The Player's Drive)**: The feedback feeds into a larger long-term goal. The player gains experience, gets closer to a boss, or earns currency to buy better gear, which drives them back to perform more actions.

### Example: Diablo's Core Loop
- **Action**: Click on enemies to attack them.
- **Feedback**: Enemies drop loot and experience points.
- **Motivation**: Equip better gear to fight stronger enemies, driving the player to repeat the action.

---

## Why Core Loops Fail

When a game feels "boring" or "grindy", it is almost always a failure of the core loop. This usually happens in one of three ways:

* **Mismatched Actions**: The actual mechanical input is frustrating, unresponsive, or repetitive without skill.
* **Muted Feedback**: The action doesn't feel satisfying. Without "game juice" (screen shake, impact sounds, particles), the brain doesn't register the reward.
* **Weak Motivation**: The long-term rewards are meaningless. If getting a legendary sword doesn't change how you play, why click on a thousand more monsters?
